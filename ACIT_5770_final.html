3. Week 9 Tutorial: Liability, Contracts, and Agreements

Essential Contract Elements

Offer

An offer is when one party shows a real intention to enter into a contract on certain terms. It must be clear enough that a reasonable person would see it as a real offer, not just casual interest.

Acceptance

Acceptance means the other party agrees to the offer clearly and unconditionally. If the terms are changed, that is usually a counter-offer, not acceptance.

Consideration

Consideration is the value exchanged between parties. This is often money, but it can also be another benefit, service, or promise to refrain from an action.

Related Terms

  • Responsibility: being responsible morally, legally, or practically
  • Accountability: being answerable for actions and willing to explain them
  • Liability: legal obligation or legal responsibility
Example question: A software company offers to build a website for $5,000. The client replies, “I accept, but only if you include hosting for free.” Is there a contract?
Model answer: No final contract yet. This is not unconditional acceptance. The client changed the terms, so this becomes a counter-offer.

4. Week 10 Tutorial: Information Privacy

What Privacy Means

Privacy relates to access, physical proximity, and knowledge about a person. The slides describe privacy as a zone of inaccessibility. Privacy is presented as a prudential right, meaning society recognizes privacy because doing so benefits society.

Important Privacy Laws

  • GDPR: applies broadly to organizations dealing with personal data of people in the EU
  • PIPEDA: applies to many private-sector organizations in Canada involved in commercial activity
  • PIPA (BC): applies to private-sector organizations in British Columbia that collect, use, or disclose personal information

Common Privacy Principles

  • Accountability: who is responsible?
  • Identifying Purposes: why is the information collected?
  • Consent: does the person need to agree?
  • Limiting Collection: what data can be collected?
  • Limiting Use, Disclosure, and Retention: how can it be used and how long can it be kept?
  • Safeguards: what security protections must be in place?
Example question: A BC company collects customer names, emails, and purchase history. What privacy ideas should it consider?
Model answer: It should identify the purpose of collection, get proper consent where required, limit collection to what is necessary, protect the information with safeguards, and follow retention and disclosure limits.

5. Week 12 Tutorial: Computer Reliability

Three Causes of Faulty Output

  1. Bug in the program
  2. Incorrect data input
  3. Query entered incorrectly

Common Thread

Computer output is not automatically trustworthy. Problems can come from software, data, or the user’s request. Reliability depends on both technical quality and human use.

How to Manage Reliability

  • test programs carefully
  • validate inputs
  • train users
  • design clear interfaces
  • review outputs and logs
Example question: Why can a computer system generate wrong output even if the hardware is fine?
Model answer: Because wrong output can still come from a program bug, bad data input, or an incorrectly entered query.

6. Week 13 Tutorial: Networked Communications

Ethical Hacking

The review highlights the Five Phases of Ethical Hacking and asks what makes hacking ethical. Two key ideas are:

  • Permission
  • Feedback

So hacking is ethical when it is authorized and when the results are used to improve security rather than cause harm.

Security Threats

The slides also show outside threats and inside threats. A major exam idea is that organizations now use connected systems in broader environments, so security risks come from both internal and external sources.

Example question: What makes hacking ethical?
Model answer: Hacking is ethical when it is done with permission and when the results are reported back as feedback to improve security.

7. Week 14 Tutorial: Encryption, Interception, and Quantum Issues

Quantum Concepts to Know

  • Quantum computing: computing based on quantum principles
  • Quantum supremacy: when a quantum computer can do a task impractical for classical computers
  • Quantum cryptography: secure communication using quantum principles
  • Quantum-safe encryption: encryption designed to remain secure in a quantum future

Ethical and Practical Questions

  • What threats do these technologies protect against?
  • How do professionals know when to use them?
  • Is it ethical to have unbreakable codes?
  • What happens to security when quantum computing becomes practical?
  • Is it ethical for one company or country to control this technology?
Example question: Is it ethical to have unbreakable encryption?
Model answer: There are arguments on both sides. Strong encryption protects privacy and security, but it may also make lawful investigations harder. The ethical issue is balancing privacy with public safety.
