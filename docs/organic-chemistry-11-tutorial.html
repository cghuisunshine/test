1) How to study organic chemistry (efficiently)

What to focus on

  • Patterns: functional groups behave in predictable ways.
  • Cause & effect: polarity, bond strength, and stability explain reactions.
  • Representation: learn to translate between names, formulas, and structures.

Daily routine

  • 10 minutes: naming practice (small, frequent).
  • 10 minutes: reaction flashcards (reagent → product + condition).
  • 10 minutes: mechanism sketches (arrows + why it happens).

Organic chemistry is not “memorize everything.” It is “learn a few rules, then apply them consistently.”

2) Bonding & structure

2.1 Carbon’s special role

  • Carbon has 4 valence electrons and forms 4 covalent bonds.
  • Carbon forms long chains and rings because C–C bonds are strong and carbon atoms bond to each other easily (catenation).
  • Carbon can form single, double, and triple bonds, creating many structures.

2.2 Lewis structures and line structures

  • Lewis structure: shows all atoms and bonds (useful for beginners).
  • Condensed structural formula: e.g., CH3CH2CH3.
  • Skeletal (line) structure: carbon atoms are implied at line ends and corners; hydrogens on carbon are implied.

2.3 Polarity and intermolecular forces (IMFs)

Many Grade 11 questions boil down to “why does this boil higher?” or “why is it soluble in water?”

Force Where it comes from Typical examples Effect
London dispersion Temporary dipoles (all molecules) Alkanes, nonpolar molecules Stronger with larger surface area and mass
Dipole–dipole Permanent dipoles in polar molecules Ketones, aldehydes, alkyl halides Raises boiling point vs. similar nonpolar molecules
Hydrogen bonding H bonded to N, O, or F interacting with lone pairs Alcohols, carboxylic acids, amines (weaker) Often gives high boiling point & water solubility

“Like dissolves like”: polar substances dissolve in polar solvents (like water); nonpolar substances dissolve in nonpolar solvents (like hexane).

3) Hydrocarbons

Hydrocarbons contain only carbon and hydrogen.

3.1 Alkanes (single bonds)

  • General formula (acyclic): CnH2n+2
  • Names: end in -ane (methane, ethane, propane…)
  • Reactivity: relatively unreactive; combust; can undergo substitution with halogens.

3.2 Alkenes (at least one C=C)

  • General formula (one double bond, acyclic): CnH2n
  • Names: end in -ene (ethene, propene…)
  • Reactivity: the double bond is electron-rich → addition reactions are common.

3.3 Alkynes (at least one C≡C)

  • General formula (one triple bond, acyclic): CnH2n-2
  • Names: end in -yne (ethyne/acetylene, propyne…)
  • Reactivity: also undergo addition, often in multiple steps.

3.4 Cyclic hydrocarbons

  • Add the prefix cyclo- (cyclopropane, cyclohexane).
  • Cycloalkanes (single bonds) have formula CnH2n.

4) Functional groups

A functional group is the part of a molecule that largely determines its properties and reactions.

Functional group Example (condensed) IUPAC suffix/prefix Key properties (Grade 11 level)
Alkane CH3CH3 -ane Nonpolar; low reactivity; combustion
Alkene CH2=CH2 -ene Addition reactions at C=C
Alkyne HC≡CH -yne Addition reactions at C≡C
Alcohol CH3CH2OH -ol Polar; H-bonding; can be oxidized
Alkyl halide CH3CH2Cl chloro-, bromo-… Polar C–X bond; substitution / elimination
Aldehyde CH3CHO -al Polar carbonyl; oxidizes to carboxylic acid
Ketone CH3COCH3 -one Polar carbonyl; not easily oxidized further (Grade 11)
Carboxylic acid CH3COOH -oic acid Acidic; strong H-bonding; forms esters
Ester CH3COOCH2CH3 -oate Often fruity odor; formed from acid + alcohol
Amine CH3CH2NH2 -amine Basic (weak); can H-bond; smells “fishy”

Fast identification trick: scan for -OH (alcohol), C=O (carbonyl), -COOH (carboxylic acid), and -COO- (ester).

5) IUPAC naming (Grade 11)

5.1 Step-by-step algorithm

  1. Find the longest carbon chain that includes the main functional group or multiple bond.
  2. Number the chain to give the lowest possible number to the main feature (double bond, functional group).
  3. Name substituents (branches) and their positions: methyl-, ethyl-, chloro-, etc.
  4. Use prefixes for repeats: di-, tri-, tetra-; list substituents alphabetically (ignore di/tri for alphabet).
  5. Assemble: positions-substituents + parent + suffix. Use commas between numbers, hyphens between numbers and words.

5.2 Parent chain roots (1–10 carbons)

1 meth-   2 eth-   3 prop-  4 but-   5 pent-
6 hex-    7 hept-  8 oct-   9 non-   10 dec-

5.3 Examples

  • CH3CH2CH3 → propane
  • CH3CH(CH3)CH2CH3 → 2-methylbutane
  • CH3CH2CH=CH2 → but-1-ene
  • CH3CH2OH → ethanol
  • CH3COOH → ethanoic acid

5.4 Common naming pitfalls

  • Don’t pick the “longest-looking” chain—pick the most carbons chain that includes the key feature.
  • Number so the double bond or functional group gets the lowest number.
  • Alphabetize substituents: ethyl before methyl.

6) Isomerism

Isomers have the same molecular formula but different arrangements of atoms.

6.1 Structural (constitutional) isomers

  • Different connectivity (bonding pattern). Example: C4H10 can be butane or 2-methylpropane.

6.2 Stereoisomers (same bonds, different 3D arrangement)

  • Geometric (cis/trans) in alkenes: restricted rotation about C=C can lock groups into different positions.
  • Grade 11 rule of thumb: if each carbon of the double bond has two different groups, you may have cis/trans isomers.

Isomers often have different boiling points, smells, or reactivity, even though their formulas match.

7) Reaction types

Reaction type What happens (simple) Typical organic example
Addition Atoms add across a double/triple bond Alkene + H2 → alkane
Substitution One atom/group is replaced by another Alkane + Cl2 (UV) → chloroalkane + HCl
Elimination Small molecule is removed to form double bond Alcohol → alkene + water
Oxidation Gain O / lose H / increase bonds to O Alcohol → aldehyde/ketone → acid
Condensation Two molecules join, releasing a small molecule (often water) Acid + alcohol → ester + water
Hydrolysis Breaking a bond using water (reverse of condensation) Ester + water → acid + alcohol

7.1 A beginner-friendly idea of “mechanism”

A mechanism explains how a reaction happens step-by-step: which bonds break, which form, and why. At Grade 11, focus on:

  • Polarity (partial charges δ+ and δ−)
  • Electron-rich areas (double bonds, lone pairs)
  • Electron-poor areas (carbon attached to electronegative atoms)

8) Core reactions & conditions (Grade 11 toolkit)

Note: exact conditions vary by curriculum. The goal here is recognizing the pattern: reagent + functional group → predictable product.

8.1 Combustion (hydrocarbons and alcohols)

  • Complete combustion: produces CO2 and H2O.
  • Incomplete: can produce CO and soot (C).
Example (alkane): C3H8 + 5 O2 → 3 CO2 + 4 H2O

Carbon monoxide (CO) is toxic. Never burn fuels without good ventilation.

8.2 Alkane substitution (halogenation)

  • Reactants: alkane + Cl2 or Br2
  • Conditions: UV light / sunlight
  • Products: haloalkane + HCl or HBr
CH4 + Cl2  —(UV)→  CH3Cl + HCl

This is a substitution because a hydrogen is replaced by a halogen.

8.3 Alkene addition reactions

Hydrogenation

  • Reactants: alkene + H2
  • Catalyst: Ni / Pt / Pd
  • Product: alkane
CH2=CH2 + H2  —(Ni)→  CH3–CH3

Halogen addition (decolorizes bromine water)

  • Reactants: alkene + Br2
  • Observation: orange/brown bromine water becomes colorless
  • Product: dibromoalkane
CH2=CH2 + Br2 → BrCH2–CH2Br

Hydration (making alcohols)

  • Reactants: alkene + H2O
  • Catalyst: acid (often H+)
  • Product: alcohol
CH2=CH2 + H2O  —(H+)→  CH3CH2OH

8.4 Alcohol oxidation (a key Grade 11 pattern)

Oxidation often uses an oxidizing agent such as acidified potassium dichromate (K2Cr2O7/H+).

  • Primary alcohol → aldehyde → carboxylic acid (further oxidation)
  • Secondary alcohol → ketone
  • Tertiary alcohol → resistant to oxidation (Grade 11 level)
CH3CH2OH  →  CH3CHO  →  CH3COOH

In real labs, dichromate solutions are hazardous and require proper PPE and disposal.

8.5 Esterification (condensation)

  • Reactants: carboxylic acid + alcohol
  • Conditions: concentrated acid catalyst (often H2SO4), heat
  • Products: ester + water
CH3COOH + CH3CH2OH  ⇌  CH3COOCH2CH3 + H2O

Esters often have characteristic pleasant smells (fruity), which is why they’re used in flavors and perfumes.

8.6 Ester hydrolysis (reverse reaction)

  • Reactants: ester + water
  • Conditions: acid or base; heating speeds it up
  • Products: carboxylic acid + alcohol

9) Polymers

A polymer is a very large molecule made from repeating units called monomers.

9.1 Addition polymerization (alkenes → plastics)

  • Monomers (often alkenes) join without producing a small molecule byproduct. 
n CH2=CH2  →  (–CH2–CH2–)n   (poly(ethene) / polyethylene)

9.2 Condensation polymerization (two-functional monomers)

  • Monomers join while releasing a small molecule (often water).
  • Examples: polyesters, proteins (peptide bonds), nylon (advanced).

Real-world note: polymer properties depend on chain length, branching, and cross-linking (why plastics can be flexible or rigid).

10) Lab skills & techniques

10.1 Distillation (separating liquids by boiling point)

  • Heat a mixture; the lower boiling point component vaporizes first, then condenses and is collected.
  • Fractional distillation is used when boiling points are close together.

10.2 Reflux (heating without losing reactants)

  • A condenser returns vapors to the flask, allowing prolonged heating (common in organic reactions).

10.3 Chromatography (separating by attraction)

  • Components move at different speeds based on their attraction to the stationary phase vs. the mobile phase.
  • Rf value (paper/TLC): Rf = distance traveled by spot / distance traveled by solvent front.

Safety basics: goggles, lab coat, gloves when required, no tasting chemicals, waft odors, and use a fume hood for volatile substances.

11) Practice problems (with answers)

Try the questions first. Then open the answers to check your thinking.

11.1 Quick identification

  1. Identify the functional group in CH3CH2OH.
  2. Is CH3CH2CH3 polar or nonpolar (overall)?
  3. Which has a higher boiling point: ethanol (CH3CH2OH) or propane (C3H8)?
  4. What observation indicates an alkene is present when using bromine water?
  5. Name the compound CH3CH(CH3)CH3.
Answers (Quick identification)
  1. Alcohol (hydroxyl group, –OH).
  2. Nonpolar overall (hydrocarbon).
  3. Ethanol, due to hydrogen bonding.
  4. Orange/brown bromine water becomes colorless.
  5. 2-methylpropane (isobutane).

11.2 Naming practice

  1. Name CH3CH2CH2CH3.
  2. Name CH3CH2CH=CHCH3 (assume the lowest number for the double bond).
  3. Name CH3CH(Cl)CH2CH3.
  4. Draw (or describe) the structure of 3-methylpentane.
  5. Give the molecular formula for hexane.
Answers (Naming practice)
  1. Butane.
  2. Pent-2-ene.
  3. 2-chlorobutane.
  4. Five-carbon chain with a methyl group on carbon 3: CH3CH2CH(CH3)CH2CH3.
  5. C6H14.

11.3 Reaction thinking

  1. What type of reaction is hydrogenation of an alkene?
  2. Write the products of complete combustion of butane (C4H10).
  3. What reagents/conditions convert ethene to ethanol (Grade 11 level)?
  4. Primary alcohol oxidation can produce which functional group first?
  5. What are the products of esterification between ethanoic acid and methanol?
Answers (Reaction thinking)
  1. Addition (H2 adds across the C=C).
  2. CO2 and H2O (balanced: 2 C4H10 + 13 O2 → 8 CO2 + 10 H2O).
  3. Hydration: water with an acid catalyst (H+) (industrial variations exist).
  4. An aldehyde.
  5. Methyl ethanoate (CH3COOCH3) + water.

12) Glossary (high-yield terms)

  • Functional group: reactive part of a molecule that drives properties/reactions.
  • Homologous series: family with same functional group and general formula.
  • Isomers: same formula, different structures.
  • Oxidation: gain O / lose H / increase bonds to O.
  • Condensation: joining molecules while losing a small molecule (often water).
  • Hydrolysis: breaking a bond using water (often reverse of condensation).
  • Polymer: large molecule made of repeating monomer units.
