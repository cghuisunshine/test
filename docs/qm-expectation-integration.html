1) “Is this correct?” — Checking the derivation

Verdict
As written, the steps are not correct because they mix up ordinary multiplication with the quantum inner product, and they also miss the normalization factor.

What the written steps were aiming for

Starting from the energy eigenvalue equation:

\[ \hat H \psi = E \psi \]

The next line tried to do something like “multiply both sides by \(\psi\)” and conclude: \[ \langle \psi | \hat H | \psi \rangle = E|\psi|^2 \] But that’s where the meaning changes.

Problem 1 — Inner product vs ordinary multiplication

In QM, the bra-ket expression \[ \langle \psi | \hat H | \psi \rangle \] is not the same as \(\psi \cdot \hat H \psi\). It means an integral overlap:

\[ \langle \psi | \hat H | \psi \rangle = \int \psi^*(x)\,\hat H\psi(x)\,dx \]

The left factor must be \(\psi^*(x)\) (complex conjugate), because inner products in QM use conjugation to produce real expectation values for Hermitian operators.

Problem 2 — Missing normalization

Even if \(\psi\) is an eigenstate of \(\hat H\), we get:

\[ \langle \psi | \hat H | \psi \rangle = \int \psi^*(x)\,E\psi(x)\,dx = E\int |\psi(x)|^2\,dx \]

So the correct general identity is:

\[ \boxed{\langle \psi | \hat H | \psi \rangle = E\langle \psi | \psi \rangle} \]

Only if \(\psi\) is normalized, meaning \(\langle \psi | \psi \rangle = 1\), do we get:

\[ \boxed{\langle \psi | \hat H | \psi \rangle = E} \]

Clean derivation
\[ \hat H\psi = E\psi \quad \Rightarrow \quad \langle\psi|\hat H|\psi\rangle = E\langle\psi|\psi\rangle \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{if } \langle\psi|\psi\rangle=1,\ \langle\hat H\rangle=E. \]

2) “What’s the meaning of integration?”

Core idea: integration = adding up infinitely many tiny contributions

Integration is basically a continuous version of summation. It means: “add up all the tiny pieces”.

General meaning
\[ \int f(x)\,dx \quad \text{means} \quad \text{sum of tiny pieces } f(x)\,dx \text{ over a continuum.} \]

Why integration appears in quantum mechanics

A wavefunction \(\psi(x)\) is spread out over space, so physical quantities depend on contributions from all positions, not just one point. Integration is how we combine those contributions across a continuous variable \(x\).

Probability from probability density

In position space, \[ |\psi(x)|^2 \] is a probability density, not a probability by itself. To get the probability of finding the particle in a region \([a,b]\), we integrate:

\[ P(a \le x \le b) = \int_a^b |\psi(x)|^2\,dx \]

Interpretation: each tiny interval \(dx\) contributes a tiny probability \(|\psi(x)|^2dx\), and the integral adds them all up.

Inner product as “overlap over all space”

The inner product is:

\[ \langle \phi|\psi\rangle = \int \phi^*(x)\psi(x)\,dx \]

Meaning: measure how much \(\phi\) and \(\psi\) overlap across all \(x\). If they are orthogonal, this integral becomes \(0\).

Expectation value as a continuous weighted average

The expectation value of an observable \(\hat A\) is:

\[ \langle A\rangle = \langle\psi|\hat A|\psi\rangle = \int \psi^*(x)\,\hat A\,\psi(x)\,dx \]

This is the continuous analogue of: \[ \text{average} = \sum (\text{value})\times(\text{probability}) \] but since position is continuous, the sum becomes an integral.

One-sentence takeaway
\[ \boxed{\text{Integration in QM = adding up contributions from all possible positions (or states).}} \]
