The big idea

Gibbs free energy, usually written G, is a thermodynamic “accounting function” that tells you whether a process can happen spontaneously under the common lab conditions of approximately constant temperature and pressure.

Under constant T and P:

ΔG < 0 (spontaneous)   |   ΔG = 0 (equilibrium)   |   ΔG > 0 (nonspontaneous)

This is a criterion for direction, not speed. A reaction can have ΔG < 0 and still be slow without a catalyst.

Origin & historical context

The free energy named after Josiah Willard Gibbs (1839–1903) grew out of his foundational work on equilibrium thermodynamics. In the 1870s, Gibbs published a series of papers culminating in the landmark 1876–1878 monograph On the Equilibrium of Heterogeneous Substances.

Gibbs’s key move was to treat equilibrium as a problem in variational calculus: for a system constrained by its environment (for example, fixed T and P), equilibrium is found by minimizing an appropriate thermodynamic potential. The function we now call Gibbs free energy became the natural potential for constant-T, constant-P situations—exactly the setup for most chemistry and materials experiments.

Why “free”?

“Free energy” historically meant energy “free” to do non-expansion work under specified constraints. For Gibbs free energy, the constraints are usually constant T and P.

Related idea

Helmholtz free energy F = U − TS is the potential for constant T and constant volume, common in physics and statistical mechanics.

Definitions & meanings

You’ll see Gibbs free energy defined in several equivalent ways. Each highlights a different physical meaning.

Core definitions

G = H − TS

where enthalpy H = U + PV, internal energy is U, pressure is P, volume is V, temperature is T, and entropy is S.

G = U + PV − TS

What each term is “paying for”

  • U: microscopic energy stored in motions and interactions of particles.
  • PV: the “cost” to make room against an external pressure (expansion work).
  • TS: energy effectively “tied up” by thermal disorder at temperature T.

Units

G is an energy. In chemistry it’s often reported as kJ/mol for molar quantities (e.g., reaction free energy). In physics it may be per system (J) or per particle.

Deriving the key criteria

The power of G comes from combining the first and second laws for a system in contact with a thermal and mechanical environment. Here’s the standard path.

Start with the thermodynamic identities

For a simple compressible system (no electrical, surface, or other generalized work terms), the differential form of internal energy is:

dU = T dS − P dV

(More generally, you add terms like + μ dn for composition changes, or + E dQ for electrical work.)

Define H and then G

Enthalpy is H = U + PV, so:

dH = dU + P dV + V dP

Substitute dU:

dH = T dS + V dP

Now define Gibbs free energy G = H − TS:

dG = dH − T dS − S dT

Substitute dH:

dG = V dP − S dT

This compact result is why G is so convenient: it tells you how G changes with pressure and temperature.

The equilibrium/minimum principle

Consider a system at fixed temperature and pressure, allowed to change internally (reaction progress, phase redistribution, etc.). Under those constraints:

T, P constant ⇒ dG ≤ 0

So G decreases for spontaneous change and reaches a minimum at equilibrium.

The underlying intuition: at constant T and P, the combination H − TS is the bookkeeping that accounts for both the energy released/absorbed (ΔH) and the entropy “price” of organizing/disorganizing at temperature T (TΔS).

Chemical potential & mixtures

Real chemical systems change composition. For multicomponent systems, the full differential includes composition:

dG = V dP − S dT + Σi μi dni

The quantity μi is the chemical potential of component i:

μi = (∂G/∂ni)T,P,nj≠i

You can think of chemical potential as “free energy per mole” for adding a little of species i to the system at constant T and P.

Equilibrium conditions

  • Phase equilibrium: each species has equal chemical potential in all coexisting phases.
  • Reaction equilibrium: the weighted sum of chemical potentials satisfies the reaction stoichiometry.

Reactions: Q, K, and ΔG

For a chemical reaction at temperature T and pressure P, the free energy change depends on composition. Two formulas are especially useful.

Reaction free energy at nonstandard conditions

ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln Q

R is the gas constant, Q is the reaction quotient (built from activities), and ° indicates a chosen standard state (often 1 bar for gases, 1 M for solutes, pure solids/liquids at 1 bar).

Link to equilibrium constant

At equilibrium, ΔG = 0 and Q = K:

ΔG° = −RT ln K

This connects thermodynamics (free energies) to chemical equilibrium (K).

What does “standard” really mean?

ΔG° is not “the free energy change at 1 atm for everything” in a naive sense; it’s tied to a convention-defined reference state for each species. In precise work you use activities, not raw concentrations or partial pressures.

Free energy and useful work

Under constant T and P, the decrease in Gibbs free energy gives an upper bound on the maximum useful (non-PV) work obtainable from a process.

wuseful,max = −ΔG

“Useful work” excludes the expansion/compression work PΔV that is already accounted for in enthalpy.

Electrochemistry (batteries)

In an ideal (reversible) electrochemical cell, electrical work is the useful work, so:

ΔG = −n F E

where n is moles of electrons transferred, F is Faraday’s constant, and E is the cell potential.

How T and P change G

From dG = V dP − S dT, you can read off two important derivatives:

Pressure dependence

(∂G/∂P)T = V

Increasing pressure tends to favor states with smaller volume (think: denser phases).

Temperature dependence

(∂G/∂T)P = −S

Increasing temperature lowers G more for high-entropy states, which is why higher T often favors disorder (like melting).

Gibbs–Helmholtz relation (temperature dependence of ΔG)

A common form (for a fixed pressure) relates how G/T changes with T:

(∂(G/T)/∂T)P = −H/T2

This is useful when estimating how equilibrium constants shift with temperature via ΔG° = −RT ln K.

Worked examples

1) When does ice melt spontaneously?

For melting at 1 bar: H2O(s) → H2O(l). A simple approximation uses:

ΔG ≈ ΔHfus − TΔSfus

At the melting point (T = 273.15 K), the phases are in equilibrium, so ΔG = 0 and:

ΔSfus = ΔHfus/Tm

For T > Tm, the TΔS term dominates and ΔG < 0: melting is spontaneous. For T < Tm, ΔG > 0: freezing is spontaneous.

2) Predicting reaction direction with Q

Suppose ΔG° is known for a reaction at temperature T. You measure concentrations/pressures and compute Q. Then:

  • If Q < K, then ln(Q/K) < 0 and ΔG < 0: the reaction proceeds forward.
  • If Q = K, then ΔG = 0: equilibrium.
  • If Q > K, then ΔG > 0: the reaction proceeds in reverse.

3) Maximum electrical work from a battery

If a cell transfers n = 2 moles of electrons per mole of reaction and has E = 1.10 V, then the ideal free energy change is:

ΔG = −n F E ≈ −(2)(96485 C/mol)(1.10 J/C) ≈ −212 kJ/mol

Real cells deliver less because of irreversibilities (internal resistance, overpotentials, heat losses).

Practice problems (with short answers)

Problem Answer sketch
A process at constant T,P has ΔH = −40 kJ and ΔS = −120 J/K at T = 298 K. Is it spontaneous? ΔG = ΔH − TΔS = −40 kJ − 298(−0.120 kJ/K) ≈ −4.2 kJ. Spontaneous.
For a reaction, ΔG° = +5.7 kJ/mol at 298 K. What is K? K = exp(−ΔG°/(RT)) ≈ exp(−5700/(8.314·298)) ≈ 0.10.
At fixed T,P, why does equilibrium correspond to a minimum of G rather than a maximum? Because spontaneous changes require dG ≤ 0. The system “rolls downhill” until no further decrease is possible.

Common pitfalls

  • Confusing ΔG and ΔG°: the first depends on current conditions (Q); the second is tied to a standard state.
  • Mixing up “spontaneous” and “fast”: kinetics controls rate; thermodynamics controls direction and limits.
  • Using concentration instead of activity in precise equilibrium calculations (especially for ions).
  • Forgetting the constraint: the criterion ΔG < 0 is specifically for constant T and P. Other constraints use other potentials (e.g., Helmholtz free energy at constant T and V).
  • Sign convention slips: keep units consistent (J vs kJ) and remember that ΔG = ΔH − TΔS.

Summary

  • G = H − TS = U + PV − TS
  • dG = V dP − S dT + Σ μ dn
  • At constant T,P: spontaneous change makes G decrease.
  • ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln Q
  • ΔG° = −RT ln K
  • wuseful,max = −ΔG
