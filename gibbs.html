ABig Picture & Origin Why G exists
Big idea constant T,P

Gibbs free energy \(G\) is the thermodynamic potential that is most useful when the surroundings effectively hold \(T\) and \(P\) constant (typical chemistry lab conditions).

Math View

Define \(G = H - TS = U + PV - TS\). For a system that can exchange heat with a temperature reservoir and do only \(PV\) work against a pressure reservoir, spontaneous change at fixed \(T,P\) satisfies \(\Delta G \le 0\), and equilibrium satisfies \(\Delta G = 0\).

Intuition View

At constant temperature and pressure, nature “wants” the total entropy of system + surroundings to increase. \(G\) is a re-packaging of that rule into a quantity you can compute using system properties and tabulated data.

Historical motivation equilibrium prediction

What problem it solved: predicting chemical equilibrium, phase equilibrium, and useful work under realistic constraints.

Math View

Thermodynamics starts naturally with \(U(S,V,\{n_i\})\). But experiments often control \(T\) and \(P\), not \(S\) and \(V\). Using a Legendre transform produces a potential with natural variables \((T,P,\{n_i\})\), namely \(G\).

Intuition View

Instead of tracking “how much heat in a reversible sense” (\(S\)) and the system’s volume, we switch to the knobs we can hold steady: temperature and pressure.

What it extends/unifies 1st + 2nd laws

Key unification: energy conservation (First Law) plus entropy production (Second Law) become one downhill criterion at fixed \(T,P\).

Math View

Under stated conditions, \(\Delta G = -T\Delta S_{\text{univ}}\). Since \(\Delta S_{\text{univ}}\ge 0\), we get \(\Delta G \le 0\) for spontaneous change.

Intuition View

“Downhill in \(G\)” is another way to say “the universe’s entropy increases,” but written in a lab-friendly form.

A bookkeeping function that measures how “driving force” changes when temperature and pressure are held fixed.

Plain-language
The free-energy “price” per mole of species \(i\).
Physical meaning
Species move or react in ways that tend to reduce the appropriate potential differences in \(\mu_i\).
Units
J/mol.
Validity
Well-defined for equilibrium states; in mixtures depends on composition via activities.

For reaction stoichiometry \(\sum_i \nu_i A_i = 0\), define \(dn_i = \nu_i\,d\xi\).
Plain-language
A single knob that measures how far the reaction has proceeded.
Units
mol.
Why it helps
It lets you write \(dG = \Delta_r G\,d\xi\) at constant \(T,P\), linking reaction direction to the sign of \(\Delta_r G\).
“Why does the entropy term have T in it?” why ×2

Math View

Entropy has units J/K, so to subtract it from an energy you must multiply by \(T\) (K) to get J. Also, the reservoir relation \(\Delta S_{\text{surr}}=-q/T\) makes \(T\) the conversion factor between heat flow and entropy change.

Intuition View

At higher temperature, the “preference” for spreading energy (entropy) matters more. The same entropy gain is a bigger deal at high \(T\).

CEquations, Laws, or Theorems how to use G
Core definition state function
Assumptions: equilibrium state functions; \(T\) in kelvin.
\[G = H - TS = U + PV - TS\]
Interpretation
Balances enthalpy (energetic cost/benefit) against entropy (dispersal) weighted by \(T\).
Sanity checks
Units: \(TS\) is J; special case \(T\to 0\) gives \(G\to H\).
Differential form simple compressible system
Assumptions: only pressure–volume work; equilibrium; chemical potentials exist.
\[dG = -S\,dT + V\,dP + \sum_i \mu_i\,dn_i\]

Math View

At constant \(T,P\), \(dG=\sum_i \mu_i\,dn_i\). For fixed composition, \(\left(\partial G/\partial T\right)_P=-S\) and \(\left(\partial G/\partial P\right)_T=V\).

Intuition View

If you heat the system, \(G\) drops at a rate set by how much entropy it has. If you compress it (higher \(P\)), \(G\) rises proportionally to its volume.

Spontaneity at constant T,P criterion
Assumptions: system exchanges heat with a \(T\) reservoir and does only \(PV\) work against a \(P\) reservoir.
\[\Delta G \le 0\ \text{(spontaneous)},\qquad \Delta G = 0\ \text{(equilibrium)}\]

Math View

Under these conditions, \(\Delta G = -T\Delta S_{\text{univ}}\). Since \(\Delta S_{\text{univ}}\ge 0\), spontaneous change implies \(\Delta G\le 0\).

Intuition View

The system can lower \(G\) by either reducing enthalpy (release heat) or by increasing entropy enough that the surroundings “approve” the change at that temperature.

Composition dependence: Q and K equilibrium
Assumptions: activities used (ideal gases/solutions are special cases); same standard state throughout.
\[\Delta_r G = \Delta_r G^\circ + RT\ln Q\]
\[\Delta_r G^\circ = -RT\ln K\]
Symbols
\(Q=\prod_i a_i^{\nu_i}\) (dimensionless), \(R\) gas constant, \(T\) in K.
Direction test
If \(Q<K\) then \(\Delta_r G<0\) (forward). If \(Q>K\) then \(\Delta_r G>0\) (reverse).
Maximum useful (non-PV) work reversible
Assumptions: constant \(T,P\); reversible; separate out \(PV\) work.
\[w_{\text{nonPV,max}} = -\Delta G\]

Math View

For reversible processes, the Second Law is an equality, so the drop in \(G\) is exactly the maximum work available beyond expansion/compression work.

Intuition View

\(PV\) work is “spent” just pushing the atmosphere back. The remaining drop in free energy is what you can turn into electrical work, shaft work, etc.

Temperature dependence Gibbs–Helmholtz
Assumptions: pressure held; composition fixed appropriately; equilibrium state functions.
\[\left(\frac{\partial \Delta G}{\partial T}\right)_P = -\Delta S\]
\[\left(\frac{\partial (\Delta G/T)}{\partial T}\right)_P = -\frac{\Delta H}{T^2}\]

Math View

The slope of \(\Delta G(T)\) is minus entropy change; this is a unit-consistent statement because \(S\) is J/K, matching J per K.

Intuition View

If a process increases entropy, raising \(T\) makes it more favorable (more negative \(\Delta G\)).

DDerivations or Proofs step-by-step
D1) Derive the differential form of G Legendre transform
Assumptions: simple compressible system; only \(PV\) work; equilibrium; reversible differential form used for the fundamental equation.
  1. Start from the fundamental relation (definition-level result).
    For a simple compressible system with composition changes:
    \[dU = T\,dS - P\,dV + \sum_i \mu_i\,dn_i\]

    Math View This is a differential statement of the First Law plus the definition of temperature and pressure as conjugate variables.

    Intuition View Energy changes via heat-like entropy changes, expansion work, and adding/removing matter.

  2. Define Gibbs free energy. 
    \[G \equiv U + PV - TS\]
    This is a Legendre transform that swaps natural variables from \(S,V\) to \(T,P\).
  3. Differentiate using the product rule. 
    \[dG = dU + P\,dV + V\,dP - T\,dS - S\,dT\]
  4. Substitute the expression for \(dU\) and simplify. 
    \[dG = (T\,dS - P\,dV + \sum_i \mu_i\,dn_i) + P\,dV + V\,dP - T\,dS - S\,dT\]
    Cancel like terms: \(T\,dS\) cancels, and \(-P\,dV\) cancels with \( +P\,dV\).
  5. Final result. 
    \[dG = -S\,dT + V\,dP + \sum_i \mu_i\,dn_i\]

Quick check

Units: \(S\,dT\) is (J/K)(K)=J; \(V\,dP\) is (m³)(Pa)=J; \(\mu\,dn\) is (J/mol)(mol)=J.

Intuition View

Changing temperature changes the weight of entropy, changing pressure changes the “cost” of volume, and changing composition changes free energy through chemical potentials.

D2) Show that ΔG = −TΔS_univ at constant T,P (PV-only) criterion
Assumptions: surroundings act as large reservoirs at constant \(T\) and constant external \(P\); only \(PV\) work; “universe” = system + surroundings is isolated.
  1. Start from the Second Law for the universe. 
    \[\Delta S_{\text{univ}} = \Delta S_{\text{sys}} + \Delta S_{\text{surr}} \ge 0\]
  2. Relate surroundings entropy change to heat at constant \(T\).
    Because the surroundings are a thermal reservoir at temperature \(T\):
    \[\Delta S_{\text{surr}} = -\frac{q_{\text{sys}}}{T}\]

    Math View Reservoir entropy change is \(\Delta S = q/T\); sign is opposite because heat leaving system enters surroundings.

    Intuition View If the system releases heat, the surroundings gain entropy; if the system absorbs heat, the surroundings lose entropy.

  3. Use the First Law with only \(PV\) work against constant external pressure.
    Sign convention: work done by system is negative in the energy balance.
    \[\Delta U = q_{\text{sys}} + w_{\text{PV}}\quad\text{with}\quad w_{\text{PV}} = -P\Delta V\]
    So \(q_{\text{sys}} = \Delta U + P\Delta V\).
  4. Substitute into \(\Delta S_{\text{surr}}\). 
    \[\Delta S_{\text{surr}} = -\frac{\Delta U + P\Delta V}{T}\]
  5. Compute \(T\Delta S_{\text{univ}}\). 
    \[T\Delta S_{\text{univ}} = T\Delta S_{\text{sys}} - (\Delta U + P\Delta V)\]
  6. Recognize \(\Delta G\) at constant \(T,P\).
    From \(G=U+PV-TS\), if \(T,P\) are constant:
    \[\Delta G = \Delta U + P\Delta V - T\Delta S_{\text{sys}}\]
  7. Combine results. 
    \[\Delta G = -T\Delta S_{\text{univ}} \le 0\]

Quick check

If the universe entropy increases (\(\Delta S_{\text{univ}}>0\)), then \(\Delta G<0\) as required for spontaneity.

Intuition View

You can treat the surroundings as a “bank” that charges entropy \((q/T)\) for heat exchange. \(G\) is the net balance: enthalpy-like energy plus expansion cost minus entropy credit.

D3) Derive ΔrG = ΔrG° + RT ln Q and ΔrG° = −RT ln K activities
Assumptions: activities \(a_i\) describe nonideality; chemical potentials exist; temperature fixed.
  1. Use the standard chemical potential form. 
    \[\mu_i = \mu_i^\circ + RT\ln a_i\]
  2. Define reaction Gibbs free energy (as written). 
    \[\Delta_r G = \sum_i \nu_i\mu_i\]
  3. Substitute and separate standard and composition terms. 
    \[\Delta_r G = \sum_i \nu_i\mu_i^\circ + RT\sum_i \nu_i\ln a_i\]
  4. Define the standard reaction free energy. 
    \[\Delta_r G^\circ \equiv \sum_i \nu_i\mu_i^\circ\]
  5. Combine logs into a single log (reaction quotient). 
    \[\sum_i \nu_i\ln a_i = \ln\left(\prod_i a_i^{\nu_i}\right) \equiv \ln Q\]
  6. Obtain the working equation. 
    \[\Delta_r G = \Delta_r G^\circ + RT\ln Q\]
  7. At equilibrium, set \(\Delta_r G=0\) and \(Q=K\). 
    \[0 = \Delta_r G^\circ + RT\ln K\quad\Rightarrow\quad \Delta_r G^\circ = -RT\ln K\]

Quick check

\(\ln\) requires dimensionless input, so activities (not raw concentrations) are the correct objects.

Intuition View

\(Q\) measures how product-heavy the mixture is. As products accumulate, the \(RT\ln Q\) term increases and cancels the standard driving force at equilibrium.

EWorked Examples increasing difficulty
Example 1: Find the temperature where ΔG = 0 phase equilibrium idea
Given: \(\Delta H = +6.01\text{ kJ/mol}\), \(\Delta S = +22.0\text{ J/(mol·K)}\). Assume \(\Delta H,\Delta S\) are roughly constant over the temperature range.
  1. Start from the definition. 
    \[\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S\]

    Math View This is algebraic rearrangement of \(G=H-TS\) for changes at fixed \(T\).

    Intuition View Enthalpy “pushes back” if it costs energy; entropy “pulls forward” if disorder increases.

  2. Set equilibrium condition \(\Delta G=0\). 
    \[0 = \Delta H - T\Delta S\quad\Rightarrow\quad T = \frac{\Delta H}{\Delta S}\]
  3. Convert units and compute.
    Convert \(6.01\text{ kJ/mol} = 6010\text{ J/mol}\).
    \[T = \frac{6010}{22.0}\approx 273\text{ K}\]
Common mistakes trap
  • Using \(T\) in °C instead of K.
  • Forgetting kJ → J conversion, giving \(T\) off by \(10^3\).
  • Assuming \(\Delta H,\Delta S\) constant when the question explicitly says they vary with \(T\).
Example 2: Compute K from ΔG° equilibrium constant
Given: \(\Delta_r G^\circ = -5.70\text{ kJ/mol}\) at \(T=298\text{ K}\). Use \(R=8.314\text{ J/(mol·K)}\).
  1. Use the equilibrium relation. 
    \[\Delta_r G^\circ = -RT\ln K\quad\Rightarrow\quad K=\exp\left(-\frac{\Delta_r G^\circ}{RT}\right)\]

    Math View Solve for \(K\) by exponentiating; keep units consistent.

    Intuition View \(\ln K\) scales linearly with the driving force measured in thermal units \(RT\).

  2. Convert units and compute the exponent.
    \(-5.70\text{ kJ/mol}=-5700\text{ J/mol}\) and \(RT\approx 8.314\times 298\approx 2477\text{ J/mol}\).
    \[-\frac{\Delta_r G^\circ}{RT} = \frac{5700}{2477} \approx 2.30\]
  3. Exponentiate. 
    \[K \approx e^{2.30}\approx 10.0\]
Common mistakes trap
  • Using base-10 log: the formula uses natural log \(\ln\).
  • Plugging \(R=0.08206\) without matching units (L·atm vs J).
  • Forgetting that \(K\) is dimensionless (activities).
Example 3: Decide direction using Q nonstandard conditions
Given: \(\Delta_r G^\circ = +3.0\text{ kJ/mol}\), \(T=298\text{ K}\), current \(Q=0.010\).
  1. Use the nonstandard relation. 
    \[\Delta_r G = \Delta_r G^\circ + RT\ln Q\]
  2. Compute the composition term.
    \(\ln(0.010)=-4.605\), and \(RT\approx 2477\text{ J/mol}\).
    \[RT\ln Q \approx 2477(-4.605) \approx -1.14\times 10^4\text{ J/mol} = -11.4\text{ kJ/mol}\]
  3. Combine with \(\Delta_r G^\circ\). 
    \[\Delta_r G \approx 3.0 - 11.4 = -8.4\text{ kJ/mol}\]

    Math View Negative \(\Delta_r G\) means forward direction is spontaneous at this composition.

    Intuition View The mixture is so reactant-heavy that making products is strongly favored, overcoming an unfavorable standard baseline.

Common mistakes trap
  • Comparing \(Q\) to 1 instead of to \(K\).
  • Using raw concentrations when the problem indicates nonideal behavior (activities needed).
Example 4: Connect to electrochemistry cell potential
Given: \(n=2\), \(E^\circ=1.10\text{ V}\), \(F=96485\text{ C/mol}\), \(T=298\text{ K}\).
  1. Compute standard free energy change. 
    \[\Delta G^\circ = -nFE^\circ\]
    \[\Delta G^\circ \approx -(2)(96485)(1.10)\approx -2.12\times 10^5\text{ J/mol} = -212\text{ kJ/mol}\]
  2. Estimate \(K\). 
    \[K=\exp\left(-\frac{\Delta G^\circ}{RT}\right) \approx \exp\left(\frac{2.12\times 10^5}{2477}\right)\]

    Math View The exponent is ~85, so \(K\) is astronomically large.

    Intuition View A strong positive cell voltage corresponds to an enormous thermodynamic push toward products.

FCommon Questions & Misconceptions fix the usual confusions
Common Trap: “ΔG < 0 means the reaction is fast.” thermo vs kinetics

Math View

\(\Delta G\) concerns initial and final equilibrium states (a state function). Rates depend on the activation barrier, often expressed via an activation free energy \(\Delta G^\ddagger\), with rate constants like \(k \propto e^{-\Delta G^\ddagger/RT}\) (transition-state theory).

Intuition View

\(\Delta G\) tells where the process ends up (downhill overall), but the speed depends on how tall the hill is in between.

Common Trap: “A catalyst makes ΔG more negative / changes K.” state function

Math View

Catalysts change the pathway and thus \(\Delta G^\ddagger\), but not the state functions \(\Delta G\) or \(\Delta G^\circ\). Since \(\Delta G^\circ=-RT\ln K\), \(K\) is unchanged.

Intuition View

It lowers the barrier, not the start and end energies. You reach equilibrium faster, but the equilibrium position is the same.

Common Trap: “ΔG° tells the direction for my mixture.” Q matters

Math View

\(\Delta_r G = \Delta_r G^\circ + RT\ln Q\). Even if \(\Delta_r G^\circ>0\), sufficiently small \(Q\) can make \(\Delta_r G<0\) (forward spontaneous), and vice versa.

Intuition View

Standard conditions are just a reference scoreboard. Your current mixture composition can strongly favor one direction.

Common Trap: “Temperature only affects rates, not spontaneity.” TΔS

Math View

\(\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S\). Changing \(T\) directly changes the sign and magnitude of \(\Delta G\) when \(\Delta S\neq 0\).

Intuition View

At higher temperature, nature “cares more” about entropy; processes that increase entropy become more favorable.

GExam Strategy (Short & Focused) what to do under time pressure
Fast method selection trigger words
  • “Equilibrium constant K” → use \(\Delta_r G^\circ = -RT\ln K\).
  • “Nonstandard / current conditions / reaction quotient” → use \(\Delta_r G = \Delta_r G^\circ + RT\ln Q\).
  • “Spontaneous at constant T and P” → sign test \(\Delta G\) (with validity conditions).
  • “Maximum useful work / electrical work” → \(w_{\text{nonPV,max}}=-\Delta G\) (reversible, constant \(T,P\)).
  • “Temperature where equilibrium occurs” → set \(\Delta G=0\) in \(\Delta G=\Delta H-T\Delta S\) (if allowed to treat ΔH,ΔS constant).
Mental checklist avoid traps
  • Conditions: Is the question explicitly constant \(T,P\)? Any non-\(PV\) work?
  • Units: Put \(T\) in K; convert kJ↔J; keep \(R\) consistent.
  • Standard vs current: Are you asked for \(\Delta G^\circ\) or \(\Delta G\)?
  • Logs: Use \(\ln\), not \(\log_{10}\) unless stated.
  • Q/K: Ensure \(Q\) built from activities/partial pressures as required.
Common exam traps classic
  • Using °C instead of K in any expression involving \(T\).
  • Mixing \(R=8.314\) J/(mol·K) with ΔG in kJ/mol.
  • Claiming “negative ΔG means fast” (confusing thermo with kinetics).
  • Forgetting that activities make \(Q\) and \(K\) dimensionless.
HSummary & Connections big takeaway
One-paragraph summary memorize

Gibbs free energy is defined by \(G=H-TS\). For systems exchanging heat and doing only \(PV\) work with an environment at constant temperature and pressure, spontaneous change decreases \(G\) (\(\Delta G\le 0\)), and equilibrium occurs at a minimum of \(G\) (\(\Delta G=0\)). In chemistry, composition enters through chemical potentials, giving \(\Delta_r G=\Delta_r G^\circ+RT\ln Q\) and \(\Delta_r G^\circ=-RT\ln K\), which connect thermodynamics directly to equilibrium constants.

Connections other potentials
  • Helmholtz free energy \(A=U-TS\): natural for constant \(T,V\) (e.g., some physics/stat mech contexts).
  • Chemical potential \(\mu_i\): slopes of \(G\) vs composition; drives diffusion and reactions.
  • Phase equilibrium: equality of chemical potentials across phases; temperature where \(\Delta G=0\) marks coexistence for a phase change at given \(P\).
  • Electrochemistry: \(\Delta G^\circ=-nFE^\circ\) connects cell voltage to equilibrium and work.
Real-world applications where you see it
  • Predicting whether reactions proceed and where equilibrium lies (industrial synthesis, buffers, solubility).
  • Battery and fuel-cell feasibility and maximum electrical work.
  • Biochemical energetics (ATP hydrolysis as a free-energy currency).
  • Material stability and phase diagrams (why certain phases form at given \(T,P\)).
